FAMILY HISTORY
\n20 August 2019
\nBY JESSICA MORGAN<\/p>\n

Family trees often have a kaleidoscope of different people who led fascinating, inspiring, and sometimes heartbreaking lives. If you\u2019d like an idea of the many types ancestors you can discover on your tree, here\u2019s a list of nine kinds of ancestors frequently found in\u00a0family trees.<\/p>\n

Also check out the Ancestry collections below, which can start you on the road of discovery for your ancestor\u2019s story.<\/p>\n

1. Irish Famine Immigrant<\/p>\n

The Irish Potato Famine started in 1845. In less than five years, more than a million Irish people died of starvation. About a million more immigrated to England, Canada, Australia, and America to escape the famine.<\/p>\n

Today many people around the world can trace their ancestry back to Ireland. Notable people like John F. Kennedy, Walt Disney, and George Clooney all have Irish ancestors.<\/p>\n

Do you have an ancestor who escaped the Famine? Look at these collections in your quest to find out:<\/p>\n

Searching for Missing Friends: Irish Immigrant Advertisements Placed in \u201cThe Boston Pilot,\u2019 1831-1920
\nNew York, Passenger and Crew Lists (including Castle Garden and Ellis Island), 1820-1957
\n2. Revolutionary War Soldier<\/p>\n

Throughout the Revolutionary War, about 230,000 soldiers and 145,000 militiamen (i.e. non-professional combatants) served in the Continental Army. Any able male between ages 16 and 55 could enlist, and was paid $6-8 per month.<\/p>\n

Those who fought in the Continental Army came from many backgrounds, from English merchants to German farmers to African Americans hoping to win their freedom.<\/p>\n

If you have colonial ties, you may have an ancestor who fought for America\u2019s freedom. Check out these collections to find out if your ancestor fought for America\u2019s independence:<\/p>\n

U.S. Revolutionary War Rolls, 1775-1783
\nU.S. Compiled Revolutionary War Military Service Records, 1775-1783
\nU.S. Revolutionary War Pensioners, 1801-1815, 1818-1872
\n3. Convict<\/p>\n

In the 18th\u00a0century, the British government sent thousands of convicts to the American colonies to free up overcrowded prisons in England. Once America gained independence, convicts were sent to penal colonies in Australia.<\/p>\n

British ships transported over 160,000 convicts to Australia between 1788 and 1868. Most of these convicts had been charged for petty crimes like theft or forgery.<\/p>\n

Do you have a\u00a0black sheep in your family? Take a look at convict transportation records and weekly newspapers (gazettes) published to report crimes and wanted criminal to find out more about your convict-ancestor:<\/p>\n

UK, Police Gazettes, 1812-1902, 1921-1927
\nIreland, Police Gazettes, 1861-1893
\nNew South Wales and Tasmania, Australia Convict Musters, 1806-1849
\nAustralian Convict Transportation Registers \u2013 Other Fleets & Ships, 1791-1868
\n4. Sailor<\/p>\n

What\u2019s more exhilarating than traveling the world? When sailing ships dominated the Atlantic and Pacific, there were many reasons for a man to try a life at sea: profit, career-advancement, or simply adventure.<\/p>\n

Prospective sailors could join the Navy (the arm of a nation\u2019s military that operated at sea), or the Merchant Navy (mercantile fleets owned by governments or private shipping companies).<\/p>\n

Explore these collections to maybe discover if one of your ancestors sailed the seas:<\/p>\n

UK Royal Naval Seamen Index 1853-1872
\nU.S. Deaths in US Naval Service, 1861-1867
\nUK Apprentices Indentured in Merchant Navy, 1824-1910
\nIndexes to Seamen\u2019s Protection Certificate Applications and Proofs of Citizenship
\n5. Royalty<\/p>\n

If you have colonial ancestors,\u00a0you might have British king and queens in your family tree. Over 600 colonists who settled in America have been confirmed as descendants of royalty.<\/p>\n

Many high-status colonists were the younger sons of aristocratic families in England. By law they could not inherit land, so they struck out for America for better opportunities. Their aristocratic families often trace back to medieval royalty through female lines or children born out of wedlock.<\/p>\n

Ancestry has databases for the 600 colonists with royal ties and for\u00a0Burke\u2019s Peerage, which catalogues families of nobility in England. Find out if you could descend from royalty:<\/p>\n

The Royal Descents of 600 Immigrants
\nBurke\u2019s Family Records (Indexed)
\nBurke\u2019s Peerage and Baronetage Genealogical and Heraldic Dictionary<\/p>\n
